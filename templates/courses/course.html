Translanguaging & Code-switching

Linguistic hybridity can be simply defined as how dynamic new linguistic practices are used in daily life, such as mixing languages for better communication (Translanguaging). As Defined by Hopkyns, Zoghbor, and Hassall (2020), translanguaging is the mixing of languages to aid understanding. While code-switching refers narrowly to swapping between two or more systems of languages (Hymes, 1974), translanguaging refers more broadly to the implications of bilingualism for certain purposes such as maximizing communication potential, so it includes, but it is not limited to, code-switching (Garcia, 2009b).

Edutopia's teachings

The respected award-winning organization, edutopia.org, suggests 4 interesting ways of making classrooms more inclusive for multilingual learners, which can be employed in educational settings from elementary school to undergraduate settings and beyond.

1. Incorporate your students’ language in the classroom: One example is pairing students with classmates who speak the same language. This can help them further their reasoning on that idea, considering that those students are experts in their language and reasoning may be easier for them in that language.

2. Be Dramatic and Visual: To those newly introduced to a new language, make sure as a teacher or professor to make learning more appealing and visually easy to understand. It may be hard for students to understand concepts simply by listening.

3. Provide Learning Resources that Reflect different realities: Your students come from different realities. Enabling them to learn while connecting to their previous experience will aid their learning.

Then, your goal as an educator may be to "extend their [students] first language--not replace it." It is a challenging task to incorporate such practices, but the learning results may yield fantastic results in the future.
